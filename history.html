History

The directors had bought a piece of land near Senior East Africa Portland Staff Quarters in early 90's and constructed a residential home so that the family could move from Nairobi and settle in Athi River township. This was not to be since our children, who were young were going to schools in Nairobi and there were no schools in Athi River, at that time, of similar standards. We were debating to rent out the house but for unknown reasons we not so keen in looking for a tenant. House (see photo below) remained unoccupied for over 15 years.

Later, the next plot that had an almost complete residential house came up for sale and we bought it towards end 1999. Now we had two plots of about half an acre. As we were completing the residential house, one of the directors opted to voluntarily retire from the Multinational organisation she was working when that organisation was being re-engineered. She had this final dues pay cheque and did not know what to do with this amount. At that time (late 90's), if you had money, the trend was either set up a fish and chips eatery, a mini-super market shop or a bakery. We opted for a bakery since if at home we could bake bread using a cooker oven then it was not am strange world we were moving into. We believed that the only difference was that now we were using a bigger oven! How wrong we were. We bought a jua kali mixer and oven since we could not afford new and branded ones.

Now the challenge was to find an experienced baker and a shop outlet. Since we wanted to bake and sell bread for high end market – twisted bread, it was not easy to find a Baker that had the experience. Eventually, we got one from Nakuru at a higher salary and also wanted housing near the bakery since the plan was to bake 24/7. Luckily, the father of one of the Directors had a commercial building in Athi River and he not only offered premises for baking but also a unit within the building that would serve as a flat for the Baker at no cost! He wanted the business to take off before charging us rent. We ran the business for about 6 months before we closed shop. There were myriads of problems – power black outs, Baker every week end going to see family in Nakuru, losses incurred due to the Assistant Baker not well experienced to cover for main Baker whilst away, the expected high end market was very small, unaccounted for sales proceeds etc.

What next after closure? After soul searching, we decided to convert the buildings on one of our two plots into a school- nursery. Although we had zero experience in running a school, we made sure we engaged two teachers that had a lot of experience in running a Nursery. They advised us to use the Montessori style of teaching, which was unheard of in Athi river schools. This eventually paid off. We started with one child who was later joined by two more – one from the person who has supplied sand for building our house and the other from his friend. We ran the school with three children for close to three months before the school started to pick up at a slow pace. Our mode of transport was a private saloon car (see photo below) that was used to pick and drop these children at their homes (there were no picking point alongside the road!). The driver was one of the directors, who had to drive to and fro from Nairobi to Athi River on Mondays to Fridays. Where are those three pioneer students? One, is Estana Kabibi, the other was Nicole Muthuma while the third one is Lynn.

Through hard work and a lot of patience the school has grown over the years and currently has a student population of over 500 with a transport fleet of 5 buses (no more private saloon car!) and a staff establishment of 55.
